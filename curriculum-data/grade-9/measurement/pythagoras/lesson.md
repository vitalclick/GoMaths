# The Theorem of Pythagoras

In any **right-angled triangle**, the square of the longest side (the
**hypotenuse**, opposite the right angle) equals the sum of the squares of
the other two sides:

$$a^2 + b^2 = c^2$$

where $c$ is the hypotenuse and $a$, $b$ are the two shorter sides.

## Finding the hypotenuse

When you know the two shorter sides, add their squares and take the square
root.

**Example:** the shorter sides are $3$ and $4$.

$$c = \sqrt{3^2 + 4^2} = \sqrt{9 + 16} = \sqrt{25} = 5$$

## Finding a shorter side

When you know the hypotenuse and one shorter side, **subtract** the
squares.

**Example:** the hypotenuse is $13$ and one side is $5$.

$$b = \sqrt{13^2 - 5^2} = \sqrt{169 - 25} = \sqrt{144} = 12$$

## Useful Pythagorean triples

These whole-number triangles appear often — recognising them saves time:

- $3, 4, 5$
- $5, 12, 13$
- $8, 15, 17$
- $6, 8, 10$ (a doubled $3,4,5$)

## Two things to remember

1. **Square first, then add or subtract — square-root last.**
2. The hypotenuse is always the **longest** side, opposite the right
   angle. If the hypotenuse is given, you **subtract**.
