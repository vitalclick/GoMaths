# Factorisation

Factorising is the reverse of expanding: we rewrite an expression as a
**product** of simpler factors. It is one of the most useful skills in
algebra — it lets us simplify fractions and solve quadratic equations.

## 1. Common factor

Look for a factor that appears in **every** term, then take it out.

**Example:** factorise $6x + 9$.

The highest common factor of $6x$ and $9$ is $3$:

$$6x + 9 = 3(2x + 3)$$

Always check by expanding: $3(2x + 3) = 6x + 9$. ✓

## 2. Difference of two squares

If an expression is one perfect square subtracted from another, use:

$$a^2 - b^2 = (a - b)(a + b)$$

**Example:** factorise $x^2 - 16$. Since $16 = 4^2$:

$$x^2 - 16 = (x - 4)(x + 4)$$

> A **sum** of squares such as $x^2 + 16$ does **not** factorise over the
> real numbers.

## 3. Trinomials $x^2 + bx + c$

Find two numbers that **multiply to $c$** and **add to $b$**.

**Example:** factorise $x^2 + 5x + 6$.

We need two numbers with product $6$ and sum $5$ — that is $2$ and $3$:

$$x^2 + 5x + 6 = (x + 2)(x + 3)$$

## Checklist

1. Always take out a **common factor first**.
2. Then check for a **difference of squares**.
3. Then try a **trinomial** pair.
4. **Expand your answer** to confirm it matches the original.
