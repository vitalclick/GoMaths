# Solving Linear Equations

A **linear equation** is an equation where the unknown (usually $x$) appears only to the power of 1 — no squares, no cubes, no roots.

The goal when solving is always the same: **isolate the unknown on one side of the equals sign.** To do that, we apply the same operation to both sides of the equation, keeping it balanced.

## Worked example 1

Solve for $x$:

$$2x + 5 = 13$$

**Step 1.** Subtract 5 from both sides to remove the constant on the left:

$$2x = 8$$

**Step 2.** Divide both sides by 2 to isolate $x$:

$$x = 4$$

**Check:** $2(4) + 5 = 13$ ✓

## Worked example 2 — variables on both sides

Solve for $x$:

$$3x - 7 = 2x + 5$$

**Step 1.** Subtract $2x$ from both sides:

$$x - 7 = 5$$

**Step 2.** Add 7 to both sides:

$$x = 12$$

**Check:** $3(12) - 7 = 29$ and $2(12) + 5 = 29$ ✓

## The two rules to remember

1. **Whatever you do to one side, you must do to the other.**
2. **Use the inverse operation.** To undo addition, subtract. To undo multiplication, divide.

## Common mistakes

- Forgetting to apply the operation to *both* sides.
- Sign errors when moving terms across the equals sign.
- Combining terms that aren't like (e.g., $3x + 2$ is not $5x$).
