# Simplifying Algebraic Fractions

An **algebraic fraction** has algebraic expressions on the top
(numerator) and/or bottom (denominator). We simplify it the same way we
simplify a number fraction: by cancelling factors common to both.

> **Golden rule:** you can only cancel **factors** (things multiplied),
> never individual terms (things added or subtracted).

## Step 1 — factorise top and bottom

**Example:** simplify $\dfrac{2x + 4}{2}$.

Factorise the numerator: $2x + 4 = 2(x + 2)$, so

$$\frac{2(x + 2)}{2} = x + 2$$

## Step 2 — cancel a whole common factor

**Example:** simplify $\dfrac{x^2 - 9}{x - 3}$.

Factorise the difference of squares on top:

$$\frac{(x - 3)(x + 3)}{x - 3} = x + 3$$

The bracket $(x - 3)$ cancels because it is a factor of both top and
bottom.

## The most common mistake

In $\dfrac{x^2 - 9}{x - 3}$ you **cannot** cancel the $x$'s or the $9$ and
$3$ on their own — only the complete factor $(x - 3)$ after factorising.

## Quick recipe

1. Factorise the numerator.
2. Factorise the denominator.
3. Cancel any factor that appears in both.
4. Write what remains.
